Welcome,

I am a Ph.D. candidate in Economics at George Mason University, specializing in monetary theory and the political economy of central banking. My dissertation, Whoâ€™s on First?: The Redistributive Politics of Central Banking, examines how special interests influence monetary authorities to direct new money and credit toward politically favored groups, producing Cantillon Effects. Through historical case studies of the Federal Reserve, Bank of England, and Reichsbank, I analyze the institutional, political, and economic mechanisms that drive redistributive outcomes in monetary policy.

My research agenda bridges Austrian economics, public choice theory, and monetary history, with a strong emphasis on institutional analysis. I draw on archival sources, historical narratives, and comparative case studies to understand how monetary institutions evolve under political pressure and how discretionary policy can entrench economic privilege.

I view education as an opportunity to cultivate analytical thinking and intellectual curiosity by linking core theoretical concepts to historical episodes and real-world policy debates. My approach emphasizes encouraging students to work through problems and interpret historical data to debate policy implications. I aim to make the classroom a space where students develop not only technical competence but also the ability to critically assess how institutions shape economic outcomes. By integrating clear economic reasoning with empirical evidence, I equip students to think as economists who can apply their knowledge beyond the classroom.
