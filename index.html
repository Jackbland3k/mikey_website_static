Welcome!

I am a Ph.D. candidate in Economics at George Mason University, specializing in monetary theory and the political economy of central banking. My dissertation, The Redistributive Politics of the Federal Reserve, examines how competing coalitions influence the Fed’s control over new money and credit. The first chapter traces Milton Friedman’s shift from technocratic to constitutional thinking and his recognition of the redistributive politics of money. The second interprets the Fed’s early years as a struggle between Wall Street and the farm bloc for monetary control. The final chapter—my job market paper—shows how the Fed’s discretion has enabled special interests to capture monetary policy’s redistributive effects through cases such as its first agency debt purchases, the 1970 Commercial Paper Crisis, and the 1985 Plaza Accord.

My research bridges monetary theory, political economy, and economic history to explain how central banks evolve under political pressure. I study how institutional arrangements shape who gains from monetary change, emphasizing that the redistributive effects of discretionary policy are a structural feature of central banking. Using archival sources, historical narratives, and comparative case studies, I show how these distributional dynamics influence credibility, independence, and the long-run stability of monetary regimes.

I view education as an opportunity to cultivate analytical thinking and intellectual curiosity by linking core theoretical concepts to historical episodes and real-world policy debates. My approach emphasizes encouraging students to work through problems and interpret historical data to debate policy implications. I aim to make the classroom a space where students develop not only technical competence but also the ability to critically assess how institutions shape economic outcomes. By integrating clear economic reasoning with empirical evidence, I equip students to think as economists who can apply their knowledge beyond the classroom.
